1. Why psychologists need descriptive statistics

Imagine you run your very first study:
120 students fill in an anxiety questionnaire before an important exam.
You download the data, open the file – and suddenly you are staring at a long list of numbers.

On its own, this list does not tell you much. You cannot immediately see

  • how anxious the average student feels,
  • whether there are big differences between students,
  • whether anxiety is mostly low with a few very anxious students,
  • or whether students in a “high-stress” group are actually more anxious.

Descriptive statistics are the tools that turn this raw list of numbers into a clear picture. They help you summarise, organise, and describe the data so you can understand what is going on in your sample.

Before we talk about hypotheses, p-values, or effect sizes, we always start here: with a careful descriptive look at the data. If the descriptive picture is wrong or unclear, all later conclusions become shaky.

You can think of descriptive statistics as the “language” your data speaks. Learning this language is one of the core skills of a psychologist who works with empirical findings.

2. From individual cases to an overview

Psychological data almost always come from many individuals:
students, patients, couples, families, therapy groups.

For example:

  • 120 students’ anxiety scores (0–40)
  • 80 patients’ depression scores before and after therapy
  • 200 people’s hours of sleep per night
  • 60 participants’ reaction times in a decision task

Looking at each person one by one is impossible. Instead, we ask questions at the group level:

  • What is a typical anxiety score in this group?
  • How much do scores vary between people?
  • Are extreme values present – e.g., extremely high depression scores?
  • How does one group (e.g., low vs. high stress) compare to another?

Descriptive statistics provide answers by compressing many numbers into a few meaningful summaries.

3. Variables and levels of measurement

Before summarising anything, we must understand what kind of variable we are dealing with. Different variables allow different kinds of questions and statistics.

Nominal variables – categories without order

Nominal variables place people into categories with no natural ranking.

Examples in psychology:

  • handedness: left / right
  • therapy type: CBT / ACT / psychodynamic / none
  • favourite social media platform

For nominal variables we can meaningfully talk about:

  • how many people are in each category
  • which category is most common

We cannot meaningfully compute a mean or a median of “therapy types”.

Ordinal variables – ordered categories

Ordinal variables have categories that can be ordered, but we do not know how big the steps between categories are.

Examples:

  • symptom severity: “none, mild, moderate, severe”
  • agreement with a statement: 1 = “strongly disagree” to 5 = “strongly agree”
  • satisfaction with therapy: “not at all” to “very much”

Here we can say:

  • A score of 4 means more of something than a score of 2.
  • But we do not know if the difference between 1 and 2 is the same size as the difference between 4 and 5.

For ordinal data, medians and percentiles are often more appropriate than means, because they respect the order without assuming equal distances.

Interval variables – equal steps, no true zero

Interval variables have equal units, but zero does not mean “nothing”.

The classic example is temperature in °C:
The difference between 10°C and 20°C is the same as between 20°C and 30°C, but 0°C does not mean “no temperature”.

In psychology we rarely have pure interval variables, but many scales are treated as if they were at least approximately interval. This is one reason why we often compute means and standard deviations for questionnaire scores.

Ratio variables – equal steps and a meaningful zero

For ratio variables, zero truly means “none of the quantity”.

Examples:

  • reaction time in milliseconds (0 ms = no time)
  • number of mistakes in a memory test (0 errors = perfect performance)
  • hours of sleep per night (0 hours = did not sleep)
  • anxiety scores from 0 to 40, where 0 means no reported anxiety symptoms

Most continuous psychological variables are at least approximately ratio. For ratio variables we can use all the familiar tools: means, SDs, ratios (“twice as fast”), correlations, and so on.

4. Describing distributions: not just one number

Each variable in your dataset has a distribution: it shows how often different values occur.

For example, if you plot the frequency of anxiety scores from 0 to 40, you might see:

  • many students around 18–22 points,
  • fewer students with very low or very high anxiety,
  • perhaps a single very anxious student around 35.

This shape matters. Two variables can have the same mean but very different distributions. Knowing the distribution helps you decide:

  • which summary statistics are appropriate,
  • whether the data meet assumptions for later methods,
  • whether a few extreme values are driving the results.

In descriptive statistics, we often describe distributions in words:

“Anxiety scores were roughly symmetric and centred around 20, with most students scoring between 15 and 25. Only a few students reported very low or very high anxiety.”

Being able to write such sentences clearly is a core skill for psychological reporting.

5. Central tendency: describing the “typical” value

We now turn to measures of central tendency — numbers that describe where the centre of a distribution lies.

Mean – the arithmetic average

The mean is what many people call the “average”:
add all scores and divide by the number of scores.

In our anxiety example, a mean of 20 would mean that, on average, students reported moderate anxiety.

Strengths:

  • uses all information in the data
  • widely used in research articles and statistics

Weakness:

  • very sensitive to outliers.
    If one student has a score of 40, the mean moves upwards, even if everyone else has moderate anxiety.

Because of this sensitivity, the mean works best for distributions that are not extremely skewed and do not contain extreme outliers.

Median – the middle observation

The median is the value that splits the distribution in half:
50% of scores are below, 50% are above.

If the median anxiety score is 19, half of the students have anxiety 19 or lower, and half have 19 or higher.

Strengths:

  • robust to extreme values (one very anxious student does not change the median much)
  • works well for skewed or ordinal data

Weakness:

  • less sensitive to subtle changes in the whole distribution

Mode – the most frequent value

The mode is the value that occurs most often.

This is especially useful for categorical variables:

  • “CBT” might be the modal therapy type
  • “Instagram” might be the most frequent social media platform

For continuous variables, the mode is less often reported in psychology.

Comparing mean and median

The relationship between mean and median already tells us something about the distribution:

  • mean ≈ median → distribution is roughly symmetric
  • mean > median → distribution is right-skewed (few very high scores)
  • mean < median → distribution is left-skewed (few very low scores)

When writing about your data, you can integrate this information:

“The mean and median anxiety scores were very similar (20.1 vs. 20.0), suggesting a roughly symmetric distribution without strong skewness.”

6. Variability: how different are people from each other?

Knowing the centre is important, but in psychology differences between individuals are often just as interesting. Do people cluster tightly around the mean, or are they spread out?

Range – simplest but fragile

The range is the difference between the largest and smallest observed value.

For anxiety from 6 to 33 points, the range is 27.

Problem: it depends only on two observations and is extremely sensitive to outliers. One unusual person can change the range completely.

Interquartile range (IQR) – spread of the middle 50%

To get a more robust measure, we look at the middle 50% of the data:

  • the 25th percentile (Q1) – where 25% of scores are below
  • the 75th percentile (Q3) – where 75% of scores are below

The IQR is Q3 − Q1.

If Q1 = 16 and Q3 = 23, then IQR = 7.
This means that the middle half of students have anxiety between 16 and 23.

IQR is robust and works well even when a few extreme values exist.

Standard deviation (SD) – typical deviation from the mean

The standard deviation is the most widely used measure of variability in psychology. Conceptually:

  • it tells you how far, on average, individual scores are from the mean.

If the mean anxiety score is 20 and SD is 5, a typical student is about 5 points away from the mean. Many students will fall in the range 15–25.

A larger SD means more variability:
students differ more strongly from one another.

In research reports you often see sentences like:

“Anxiety scores were moderate, M = 20.1, SD = 5.3.”

Here, M is the mean and SD is the standard deviation.

7. Putting descriptives into words

A huge part of statistical skill is the ability to translate numbers into clear, accurate sentences. Consider the following example summary:

“On a 0–40 scale, students reported moderate anxiety
(M = 20.1, SD = 5.4, Mdn = 20, IQR = 6.5).
Scores ranged from 6 to 33.
The similarity of mean and median suggests little skewness.”

This short paragraph answers four important questions:

  1. Level: Are scores low, moderate, or high?
  2. Variability: Do students differ a lot in anxiety?
  3. Extremes: What are the lowest and highest scores?
  4. Shape: Is the distribution fairly symmetric?

In your own assignments, aim to write at this level of clarity:
no unnecessary technical details, but precise enough that another reader can picture the distribution.

8. Describing and comparing groups

In psychological research we often compare groups:

  • treatment vs. control
  • low vs. high stress
  • younger vs. older adults

Descriptive statistics are the first step before any formal test.

Suppose our two groups have:

  • Low-stress: mean anxiety 20.5, SD 5.6
  • High-stress: mean anxiety 19.6, SD 5.0

At first glance, the means are very similar. If we saw this in a table or boxplot, we might write:

“Anxiety levels were similar in the low- and high-stress groups
(means around 20, SDs around 5), suggesting no strong descriptive group difference.”

This does not yet answer the inferential question “Are the groups significantly different?” – that comes later with t-tests or ANOVAs. But it already tells us that any difference we find later is unlikely to be dramatic.

Good practice in psychology:

  1. First, describe both groups (means, medians, SDs, IQRs, sample sizes).
  2. Then, and only then, move on to hypothesis tests.

9. Descriptive statistics as the base of everything that follows

Later in your studies you will encounter many statistical methods:

  • correlations and regression
  • t-tests and ANOVAs
  • confidence intervals and effect sizes
  • structural equation models and multilevel models

All of them rely on the same basic ideas:

  • what is typical in the data,
  • how much variability exists,
  • how the distribution is shaped,
  • and how groups or variables relate descriptively.

If you build a strong intuition for descriptive statistics now, every later method will make much more sense. You will be better able to:

  • spot unusual or impossible values in a dataset,
  • judge whether model assumptions are realistic,
  • read tables and figures in research articles,
  • and communicate your own findings clearly in reports and theses.

Descriptive statistics are not a boring “first chapter to survive”.
They are the core toolkit you will repeatedly use as a psychologist who works with data.

  • centre — where values tend to lie
